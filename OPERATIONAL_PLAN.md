# Fairway Golf Club — Operational Plan

How the 2,000 sq ft Proof-of-Concept (see `PRODUCT_REQUIREMENTS.md`) actually gets built, on what budget, and how it avoids the cost/timeline risk of a historic-preservation-review construction project. This is the plan for the "how do we actually get in the door and open" question — pairs with `PROJECT_BRIEF.md` (why) and `MARKETING_ROADMAP.md` (how we sell it).

## Regulatory Defense Strategy

The target site is a **historic building** on county property, currently undergoing **fast-tracked commercial leasing assessments** — meaning speed and low construction risk matter as much as the pitch itself. Two specific strategies avoid the cost and timeline risk of historic preservation review:

### Modular, Freestanding Buildout
All simulator enclosures and the sloped floor (see `PRODUCT_REQUIREMENTS.md` → Phase 4 Auto-Tee) are built as **self-contained, freestanding wood-and-metal frame pods**. They sit inside the room like furniture — no anchoring into or altering the historic envelope (walls, floors, ceiling structure). This is the key move that keeps the project out of a full historic preservation review: nothing is permanently attached to a protected historic asset.

### Macerating Plumbing (Restrooms)
Traditional restroom drains require cutting into concrete slabs — expensive, invasive, and exactly the kind of construction that triggers deeper historic-preservation scrutiny in an old building. Strategy:
1. **Prioritize sites with existing wet-wall access** where possible.
2. Where not available, use **code-compliant above-floor macerating systems** (e.g., **Saniflo**) instead of cutting concrete — avoids invasive slab work entirely while still delivering the vintage-locker-room-quality Men's/Ladies' restrooms called for in the product spec.

**Why this matters for the DEI/Storck conversation:** this isn't just a cost-saving move, it's the answer to "how do you build in a historic county building without a lengthy preservation review process" — which is very likely the county's first practical concern. See `DEI_Storck_Meeting_Prep.md`.

## Budget

**CapEx capped at ~$70,000** — a substantial reduction from the earlier $150,000 Phase 1 capital estimate in `Fairway_Golf_Club_Business_Plan.docx`. Two drivers:

1. **In-house Salesforce development.** Russ (Founder, Salesforce Application Developer) is personally designing and coding the entire customized tech backend — Golfer360 platform, booking/check-in flows, AI coaching integration — eliminating an estimated **$40,000+** in software consulting/development fees that a non-technical founder would otherwise have to pay for.
2. **Modular hardware build-out.** Freestanding pods and macerating plumbing (above) avoid the invasive, slow, expensive construction work that a from-scratch or heavily-renovated historic build-out would require.

**Target: 6-month breakeven** from physical lounge launch — longer than the original business plan's Month-3 breakeven estimate, reflecting the leaner 2-bay Proof-of-Concept scale rather than the earlier build assumption.
